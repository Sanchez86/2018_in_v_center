Everything You Need to Know about Commodities (And Even More)

Jul 28, 2016
It all started with commodities. Commodities are basic goods of the same type that can be traded on an exchange. Grain, oil, beef, cocoa and gold are just a few examples. All these have been grown or extracted somehow and there is no value added by the manufacturer. Although the quality may vary slightly depending on a producer, commodities are considered standardized goods. That’s mean that commodities of the same grade have the same price and are interchangeable. So, for example, beef raised in Europe would be sold for the same price as beef raised in Australia. That’s why commodities are sold all over the world – it doesn’t matter where they come from.

Moreover, they have a shelf life and are used by the most of the people daily. The coffee in your breakfast drink, the grain in your sandwich or the gas in your car – we all consume so many of them. That’s why changes in commodity prices influence our lives significantly and may lead to political or social and economic actions.

Major categories of commodities Since there are many commodities all of them are grouped into categories:

Agriculture The oldest and the largest commodity group includes wheat, coffee, cocoa, orange juice, rice, oats, cotton, live cattle, beef and other. As you noticed, livestock is also listed here.

Energy This category includes crude oil, natural gas, diesel, heating oil, and gasoline. Yes, this is a vital category for the world production and living.

Metals Metals include mined commodities like gold, silver, copper, platinum and less sophisticated like aluminum. Everything that might be used from jewelry to construction.

With the spread of state-of-the-art technologies, new commodities are introduced. They do not fit into traditional classification and form a category called “new commodities”. It involves nanomaterials like silicon chips, ferrosilicon, silicomanganese and others. Additionally, there are environmental commodities that include carbon emissions.

How commodities are traded Commodities are traded on the spot or futures market. In the spot market, the buyer buys and the seller sells on current price, while in the future market the future price is predetermined. The future contract gives the buyer the right (but not an obligation) to buy a certain amount of commodities for a certain price at a specific date. Naturally, most of the deals are made in the futures markets as it allows to avoid the risk of negative price changes in the future (hedging). This allows speculating on commodities price movements which lie at the heart of trading.
